# OPENCLAW — Advisory Memorandum
## Phase D — Editorial Quality & Product Transformation

---

## 1. Executive Summary

The current Lark daily monitoring output is infrastructure-valid but not yet
client-ready as a commercial intelligence product.

This is an acceptable and expected development stage. OpenClaw has already
solved or substantially stabilised the hard trust-layer problems: retrieval,
structured orchestration, citation selection, resolver, scrubber, validator,
delivery gate, citation substitution, and Lark delivery. The current weakness
is not technical integrity. The weakness is product value.

The present output reads like a lightly summarised geopolitical news digest.
A client-ready OpenClaw asset should instead read like a China exposure
intelligence product for executives, PR firms, public affairs teams, risk
teams, legal/compliance teams, and communications advisors.

The recommended product transformation is:

```
FROM:  "Daily China monitoring brief"

TO:    "Daily China Exposure Brief — executive intelligence on policy, trade,
        geopolitical, regulatory, and market developments affecting
        multinational companies."
```

The core product standard should be:

> Could a senior PR partner read this in three minutes and immediately know
> what to tell a client?

The current output does not yet meet that standard. With targeted
output-structure, editorial, and client-relevance improvements, it can.

This memo defines the problem, the required product transformation, the Phase D
scope and sequencing to get there, and the timeline relative to Phase C
completion.

---

## 2. Why This Has Been Deferred — And Why It Must Now Be Scheduled

Editorial quality has been deliberately parked during Phase 6 and Phase 7 Entry
to prioritise pipeline reliability, citation integrity, and infrastructure
(memory layer, multi-client isolation). That sequencing was correct — you
cannot improve content on an unreliable foundation.

The foundation is now solid. Continuing to defer editorial quality beyond
Phase C creates a different risk: arriving at a paid pilot gate with
infrastructure that works and output that doesn't. A client brief that isn't
client-grade isn't a pilot — it's a liability.

Phase D should be a dedicated editorial quality phase before any paid pilot
client is onboarded.

---

## 3. Current Output Assessment

The current output is suitable for pilot infrastructure monitoring but not yet
for external client delivery.

**Strengths:**

- The structure is recognisable: Executive Take, Advisory Layer, LinkedIn Draft,
  regional source sections
- It demonstrates the pipeline can generate a complete daily brief
- It includes citations and source attribution
- It surfaces cross-regional themes across US, Europe, Middle East, and
  China-related economic and geopolitical issues
- It is directionally aligned with the OpenClaw objective of a China-focused
  PR/business intelligence product

**Weaknesses:**

- It is too generic. Phrases such as "complex geopolitical and economic shifts"
  and "business leaders should adapt strategies" are broadly true but not
  sufficiently valuable. They could appear in almost any macro-geopolitical note.
- It does not clearly distinguish what changed today. The report mixes fresh
  developments with continuing context. A daily intelligence product must clearly
  identify what is new, what is continuing, and what is background.
- It does not rank importance. All Executive Take bullets feel similar in weight.
  A client needs to know which signal is most important, which is watch-only, and
  which is lower priority.
- It does not sufficiently identify client exposure. The output does not map
  developments to client functions such as Legal/Compliance, Public Affairs,
  Communications, Supply Chain, Treasury/Finance, Investor Relations, or
  sector-specific operating teams.
- The advisory layer remains too abstract. "Monitor," "review," and "consider"
  language is useful but insufficient unless tied to specific client actions,
  risk triggers, and affected functions.
- Middle East coverage risks drifting into generic energy/geopolitical news.
  Middle East items should appear only when the China linkage is explicit.
- The source list is not yet a client-grade source pack. It should become a
  structured source table with links, source type, date, region, and signal
  supported.
- The LinkedIn Draft is generic and has been observed repeating identically
  across consecutive runs (T-07, logged 2026-05-11). It should either be removed
  from the default client product or replaced with a grounded External Messaging
  Note.
- Chinese-language sources are underutilised. Run 4 (2026-05-14) recorded zero
  Chinese-language citations despite 54 Baidu results retrieved. A China
  monitoring brief that ignores Chinese-language media is not fit for purpose.
- Source authority is uneven. Citations include publishers like www.usnews.com
  alongside Reuters and the Financial Times. A client-grade brief requires
  consistent source authority standards.

## 4. Recommended Product Positioning

The product should not be sold or understood as generic daily news monitoring.

**Recommended positioning:**

> OpenClaw China Exposure Brief
>
> A daily executive intelligence product showing how China-related policy,
> trade, regulatory, geopolitical, and market developments affect multinational
> companies, communications strategy, public affairs, compliance exposure, and
> operating risk.

**Target users:**

- PR firm partners and account leads
- Public affairs teams
- Corporate communications teams
- Legal and compliance teams
- Executive office and strategy teams
- Investor relations teams
- China-facing or China-exposed multinational business units

**The product should answer five questions every morning:**

1. What changed?
2. Why does it matter?
3. Who is exposed?
4. What should the client watch next?
5. What should the client prepare, brief, review, or escalate?

## 5. Recommended Report Structure

The daily output should be restructured into the following format:

```
[YYYY-MM-DD HH:MM Shanghai | OpenClaw China Exposure Brief]

1. Executive Signal Board
2. What Changed in the Last 24 Hours
3. Client Exposure Map
4. Advisory Layer
5. Watchlist for Next 72 Hours
6. Source Pack
7. Optional External Messaging Note
```

Each section is defined below.

---

## 6. Section 1 — Executive Signal Board

Replace the current Executive Take with a decision-oriented signal board.

**Recommended format:**

```
Signal 1:
  Topic:
  What changed:
  Exposure level:
  Confidence:
  Why it matters:
```

**Recommended example:**

```
Signal 1 — US-China trade/legal retaliation
What changed: New sanctions, countermeasures, or official statements indicate
continuing escalation in legal and trade pressure.
Exposure: High
Confidence: High
Why it matters: Multinationals may face more complex compliance obligations
where US restrictions and Chinese countermeasures overlap.

Signal 2 — EU-China industrial policy tension
What changed: EU trade defence measures and concerns over Chinese industrial
overcapacity remain active.
Exposure: Medium
Confidence: Medium
Why it matters: Companies exposed to China-Europe trade may face higher
scrutiny, tariffs, procurement pressure, or public affairs risk.

Signal 3 — Middle East-China energy linkage
What changed: Middle East instability is affecting energy markets and may
influence Chinese energy demand, green technology demand, shipping costs,
and RMB/USD risk.
Exposure: Medium
Confidence: Medium
Why it matters: China-linked supply chains and energy-intensive sectors may
face cost, logistics, or market-demand effects.
```

Each Executive Signal must include: what changed, the China linkage, exposure
level, confidence level, and client relevance.

---

## 7. Section 2 — What Changed in the Last 24 Hours

**Purpose:** Make freshness visible.

The output mixes same-day developments, multi-day recall-window material, and
ongoing background without distinguishing them. This weakens the value of a
daily product.

**Recommended labels:**

- NEW TODAY
- CONTINUING
- BACKGROUND
- WATCH NEXT

**Recommended format:**

```
NEW TODAY
- Reuters reported additional sanctions activity affecting China-linked oil
  shipments, increasing compliance relevance for firms exposed to shipping,
  energy trading, and China-linked counterparties.

CONTINUING
- EU-China trade friction remains active, but today's source set does not
  show a decisive new policy move beyond the continuing trade-defence trend.

WATCH NEXT
- Monitor for Chinese ministry responses, EU trade-defence filings, new
  sanctions designations, or market reaction in RMB/oil-sensitive sectors.
```

A daily product must not make older context look like new intelligence. This
section directly increases client trust because it shows the system knows
the difference between a breaking development, an active trend, background
context, and a forward watch item.

---

## 8. Section 3 — Client Exposure Map

**Purpose:** Convert the report from news summary into decision-support work
product. This is the single highest-value upgrade in this proposal.

The report currently says "companies should monitor" but does not specify which
internal client functions should care or how they are exposed.

**Recommended format:**

```
Function / Sector:
  Exposure level:
  Issue:
  Suggested client use:
```

**Recommended example:**

```
Legal / Compliance
Exposure: High
Issue: US-China sanctions, countersanctions, export-control, or blocking-rule risk.
Suggested client use: Internal legal briefing; counterparty-screening review;
escalation to compliance owners.

Public Affairs / Communications
Exposure: High
Issue: Trade tensions, China policy narratives, industrial-policy conflict.
Suggested client use: Prepare Q&A, executive talking points, stakeholder
messaging, and non-inflammatory public posture.

Supply Chain / Procurement
Exposure: Medium
Issue: EU-China trade defence, tariffs, procurement scrutiny, overcapacity
narratives.
Suggested client use: Scenario planning for affected inputs, suppliers,
routing, or sourcing geographies.

Treasury / Finance
Exposure: Medium
Issue: RMB/USD volatility, energy-price changes, sanctions-related payment risk.
Suggested client use: Review hedging assumptions and payment/counterparty exposure.
```

Every brief should identify who inside the client organisation should care.

---

## 9. Section 4 — Advisory Layer

Retain the Advisory Layer but make it more specific, conditional, and
evidence-matched.

**Recommended formula:**

```
For [client type/function], this may matter because [business implication].
Suggested response: [low-risk action].
Watch for: [specific trigger].
```

**Recommended example:**

```
For compliance and legal teams, China-linked sanctions and countermeasure
activity may increase the risk of conflicting legal obligations across
jurisdictions. Suggested response: review whether current counterparty
screening captures China-specific retaliation and sanctions exposure. Watch
for new entity designations, export-control notices, Chinese ministry responses,
or blocking-rule signals.
```

**Style rules:**

Use conditional advisory language: "may," "could," "should review," "may wish
to monitor," "suggested response," "watch for."

Avoid unsupported alarm language: "must immediately," "urgent," "unprecedented,"
"crisis-level," "extreme," "severe" unless directly supported by cited sources.

One implication, one suggested action, and one watch trigger per bullet.

The Advisory Layer should sound like a senior consultant advising a client,
not a model summarising geopolitical risk.

---

## 10. Section 5 — Watchlist for Next 72 Hours

**Purpose:** Make the report forward-looking.

**Recommended format:**

```
WATCHLIST — NEXT 72 HOURS

1. US-China:
   Watch for Chinese official responses, new sanctions designations,
   export-control notices, or signs of resumed high-level dialogue.

2. Europe-China:
   Watch for EU trade-defence filings, tariff announcements, anti-subsidy
   actions, or Chinese retaliation signals.

3. Middle East-China:
   Watch for oil price movement, shipping disruption, China energy-security
   commentary, RMB/oil settlement signals, or sanctions tied to Chinese entities.

4. Markets:
   Watch RMB movement, Chinese equity-sector reaction, and commodity-sensitive
   sectors.
```

A valuable brief helps clients anticipate the next decision point.

---

## 11. Section 6 — Source Pack

**Purpose:** Separate the audit trail from the analysis.

The current source list is a simple regional article list. It is useful but
not yet polished. Citations inside bullets also create clutter when many
sources support one point.

**Recommended format:**

```
SOURCE PACK
# | Source | Date | Region | Source Type | Signal Supported | Link

1 | Reuters | 13 May 2026 | Middle East / China | Tier-1 international wire |
    Iran oil sanctions / China exposure | [link]

2 | CNBC | 13 May 2026 | US-China | Tier-1 business media |
    Technology / trade pressure | [link]

3 | Economist | 12 May 2026 | Europe-China | Analysis / business press |
    EU-China trade defence | [link]

4 | Sina Finance | 12 May 2026 | China market | Chinese financial press |
    RMB / market reaction | [link]

5 | CCTV | 12 May 2026 | China official | State media |
    Chinese official framing | [link]
```

**Recommended source type categories:**

1. Official / regulator
2. State media
3. Tier-1 international wire
4. Established financial/business press
5. Chinese financial press
6. Market platform / data platform
7. Commentary / analysis
8. Lower-authority platform/contributor content

Client-facing output should allow the reader to inspect the source base
quickly and independently.

---

## 12. Section 7 — Optional External Messaging Note

**Purpose:** Replace or improve the current LinkedIn Draft, which is generic
and has been observed repeating identically across consecutive runs (T-07).

**Recommended options:**

Option A — Remove from default daily client output. Use this if the pilot
client wants intelligence, not social media copy.

Option B — Replace with an External Messaging Note:

```
EXTERNAL MESSAGING NOTE

Suggested posture: Measured. Factual. Non-alarmist. Avoid overclaiming.

Possible message:
"Recent trade, sanctions, and energy-market developments show how China
exposure is no longer a single-market issue but a cross-border operating
risk. Companies with China-linked supply chains, counterparties, or market
access should monitor both policy signals and secondary effects across
Europe, the Middle East, and financial markets."

Do not say: "crisis," "urgent," "unprecedented," "severe disruption" unless
directly supported by cited source language.
```

For a PR firm, an External Messaging Note grounded in the day's specific
signals is substantially more valuable than a generic LinkedIn post.

## 13. Citation Presentation

The current citation approach is structurally sound but editorially heavy.

**Recommendation:**

- Keep internal deterministic citation enforcement unchanged
- Preserve the resolver/scrubber/validator/delivery gate architecture
- Continue using validated result_id/substitution flow internally
- In client-facing output, reduce citation clutter by citing each analytical
  bullet with concise source references and moving full link details to the
  Source Pack

**Suggested client-facing citation model:**

```
Signal 1 — US-China trade/legal retaliation [Sources: 1, 2, 5]
```

Then Source Pack provides:

```
1 | Reuters | 13 May 2026 | Title | Link
2 | CNBC | 13 May 2026 | Title | Link
5 | CCTV | 12 May 2026 | Title | Link
```

The reader should see clean analysis first and source detail second. The
system retains full validation internally.

## 14. Implementation Priorities

Improvements should be implemented in the following order within Phase D:

**Priority 1 — Freshness labelling**
Add NEW TODAY / CONTINUING / BACKGROUND / WATCH NEXT labels. Prevents stale
context from being presented as new intelligence.

**Priority 2 — Client Exposure Map**
Add function/sector exposure mapping. Converts the brief into decision-support
work product.

**Priority 3 — China-link enforcement**
Require every non-China regional item to identify its China linkage. Acceptable
China linkages for Middle East coverage include: China energy demand, Chinese
company operations, China-Middle East trade, sanctions involving China-linked
entities, RMB/USD or commodity implications, shipping or logistics effects,
green energy demand affecting Chinese firms, and multinational exposure
involving China-Middle East relations.

**Priority 4 — Source authority labelling**
Add source type categories to the Source Pack.

**Priority 5 — Source Pack with actual links**
Move full source links into a clean structured source table.

**Priority 6 — LinkedIn Draft grounding or removal**
Either remove from default pilot output or replace with a grounded External
Messaging Note. Resolves T-07.

## 15. Phase Alignment

This proposal is an editorial and product-layer improvement within the Phase 7
client-readiness workstream. It is not Phase C work. Phase C is infrastructure.

All implementation described in this memo belongs in Phase D, after Phase C
closes. Implementing output-format changes before Phase C closes would be
scope drift.

**The following improvements are appropriate within Phase D scope, subject to
operator approval at each step:**

- Modify output format instructions
- Add freshness labels to generated output
- Add Client Exposure Map section
- Add Watchlist section
- Require China linkage in each non-China regional item
- Change LinkedIn Draft to External Messaging Note or make it optional
- Add Source Pack formatting instructions
- Revise agent system prompt for advisory framing and Chinese-language source
  utilisation

**The following must not be changed without separate operator approval:**

- Retrieval providers
- Query templates
- Filtering/scoring logic
- Scrubber, validator, or delivery gate behaviour
- Runtime architecture or phase gates
- Brain Lite schema

## 18. Success Criteria

**Client value:**

1. Can a senior PR partner understand the top three signals in under three minutes?
2. Does each signal explain why it matters to a multinational company?
3. Does the brief identify which client functions are exposed?
4. Does it distinguish new developments from continuing background?
5. Does it provide a useful forward watchlist?
6. Does it include a clean source pack with actual links?
7. Does it avoid generic, reusable language?
8. Does it avoid unsupported urgency?
9. Does every non-China regional item include a clear China linkage?
10. Could the output be forwarded to a client with only light human review?

**Operational integrity:**

1. No fabricated citations
2. No uncited analytical claims
3. Validator remains GREEN
4. Scrubber and delivery gate remain unchanged unless separately approved
5. Brain Lite does not modify validation, filtering, or delivery behaviour
6. Phase discipline remains intact

## 21. Final Recommendation

Do not merely make the current Lark output prettier. Reframe the product.

The value of OpenClaw is not that it can summarise China-related news. The
value is that it can produce a source-backed, citation-controlled, daily China
exposure intelligence brief that helps PR and business clients decide what to
brief, monitor, prepare, and escalate.

The infrastructure is becoming credible. The asset becomes valuable when the
output stops behaving like a news digest and starts behaving like a
decision-support product.

**Recommended next action:** Approve this memo. Designate Phase D as a formal
phase. Begin D.1 (Brief Audit) in the final session of Phase C so Phase D
starts with findings already in hand.
